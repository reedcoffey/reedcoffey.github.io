When is it done? I think a lot of artists struggle with this questions, and I'm not any different. I've been chipping away at my latest 2'x3' canvas for a while now (see previous post), and I think I'm finally ready to call it after several revisions on the clouds and several more on the shrubs up front. Will I ever enjoy painting foregrounds? Another question I may never have a satisfying answer for. But! All things considered, I'm very happy with how this one came out. You can watch some of my visions and revisions below- each image represents an hour's work, more or less.

I took the reference photo for this painting, Dry Land Drinking, at the end of a long day's work back when I was on a trail crew. We were about halfway between Flagstaff and the Grand Canyon, working on a reroute of the Arizona Trail. You can see a bit of my handywork down in the bottom left corner of the painting! I always loved when we got some weather other than blazing heat that field season. The cool, damp air blowing in to replace the hot dust we'd been churning up all day was nothing short of magical.


It's easy to look back on field work with rose-colored glasses, but I do really miss being out in nature for ten days at a stretch for my job. You get to see so much more than on day trips or shorter camping trips because once you're out there, you're out there, no matter what the weather is like. It makes for more unique references than I've been able to get recently, that's for sure.

Speaking of rose-colored though, check out the start to my latest green painting!

I tried something similar on GREEN, and really liked the effect. On a monochromatic painting, letting the color's complement peek through from an underlying layer really helps breath life into it. I like to work with a pretty orderly palette, and layering instead of blending really helps me increase the range of colors that I can get without mixing a million different hues. It's something I'm trying to work on integrating into my practice a little bit more, so hopefully you notice it paying off in future paintings!
